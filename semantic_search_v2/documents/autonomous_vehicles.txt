Autonomous vehicles, also known as self-driving cars, use a combination of sensors, cameras, radar, and AI to navigate without human input. They represent one of the most ambitious applications of artificial intelligence and robotics technology.

Levels of vehicle automation range from zero to five. Level zero is fully manual, while level five represents full automation where the vehicle can handle all driving tasks in all conditions without any human input. Most commercially available autonomous features today fall between levels two and three.

LiDAR, which stands for light detection and ranging, is a key sensor technology used in autonomous vehicles. It works by emitting laser pulses and measuring the time it takes for them to return after bouncing off objects. LiDAR creates detailed three-dimensional maps of the vehicle's surroundings.

Computer vision uses cameras and image processing algorithms to identify objects, lane markings, traffic signs, and other features of the road environment. Deep learning has dramatically improved the accuracy of computer vision systems for autonomous driving.

Path planning algorithms determine the best route for the vehicle to follow given its current position and the positions of other objects. The algorithm must consider the rules of the road, the behavior of other vehicles and pedestrians, and the capabilities and limitations of the vehicle.

Safety is the most critical challenge in autonomous vehicle development. Edge cases, unusual situations that a human driver would handle intuitively, can confuse autonomous systems. Extensive testing in simulated and real environments is required to ensure safety.

Regulatory frameworks for autonomous vehicles are still evolving. Different jurisdictions have different rules about where and how self-driving vehicles can be tested and operated. Liability in the event of an accident involving an autonomous vehicle is a complex legal issue.

The potential benefits of autonomous vehicles include reduced traffic accidents, improved fuel efficiency, and greater mobility for elderly and disabled people. However, they also raise concerns about job displacement for professional drivers and the security of connected vehicles.
