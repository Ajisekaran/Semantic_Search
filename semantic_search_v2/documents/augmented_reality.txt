Augmented reality, or AR, overlays digital information and virtual objects onto the real world, typically through a smartphone, tablet, or specialized glasses. Unlike virtual reality, which creates a fully immersive digital environment, AR enhances the real world with digital elements.

AR technology uses sensors, cameras, and computer vision to understand the physical environment and accurately place virtual objects within it. Location data from GPS and orientation data from accelerometers and gyroscopes help position AR content in the correct position relative to the user.

Consumer applications of AR have been popularized by mobile apps and games. Pokemon Go used AR to overlay virtual creatures onto the real world, becoming a global phenomenon. AR filters and effects on social media platforms like Snapchat and Instagram are used by millions of people every day.

Retail and e-commerce are increasingly using AR to enhance the shopping experience. Customers can use AR to visualize how furniture will look in their home before buying it, or try on virtual glasses and makeup. This capability reduces returns and increases customer confidence.

Industrial applications of AR include training, maintenance, and quality control. Technicians can use AR headsets to see step-by-step instructions overlaid on the equipment they are working on. AR can guide workers through complex assembly processes, reducing errors and improving efficiency.

Healthcare is another sector exploring AR applications. Surgeons can use AR to visualize patient anatomy during procedures, improving precision and outcomes. Medical students can practice procedures on virtual patients using AR and haptic feedback systems.

Navigation and wayfinding are natural applications for AR. Rather than looking down at a map on a phone, AR navigation can overlay directions onto the real-world view. This can make navigation more intuitive and safer for both drivers and pedestrians.

The hardware for AR is still evolving. While smartphone-based AR is widely accessible, head-mounted displays offer a more immersive and hands-free experience. Companies like Microsoft with HoloLens and Apple with Vision Pro are developing dedicated AR hardware for enterprise and consumer markets.
