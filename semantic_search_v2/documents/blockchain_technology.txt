Blockchain is a distributed ledger technology that records transactions across multiple computers in a way that ensures the records cannot be altered retroactively. Each block in the chain contains a cryptographic hash of the previous block, creating a chain of blocks.

Cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and Ethereum are the most well-known applications of blockchain technology. Bitcoin was created in 2009 as a decentralized digital currency that operates without a central bank or administrator. Ethereum extended the concept with smart contracts and decentralized applications.

Smart contracts are self-executing contracts with the terms of the agreement written directly into code. They automatically execute when predetermined conditions are met, without the need for intermediaries. Smart contracts are used in decentralized finance, supply chain management, and legal agreements.

Decentralized finance, or DeFi, uses blockchain technology to recreate traditional financial services like lending, borrowing, and trading without centralized intermediaries. DeFi protocols are open-source and accessible to anyone with an internet connection.

Supply chain management is one of the most promising enterprise applications of blockchain. It provides a transparent and immutable record of the movement of goods from origin to destination. Blockchain can help prevent fraud, reduce inefficiencies, and improve traceability in supply chains.

Non-fungible tokens, or NFTs, are unique digital assets stored on a blockchain. Unlike cryptocurrencies, which are interchangeable, each NFT has unique attributes that make it distinct. NFTs have been used to represent digital art, collectibles, and in-game items.

Consensus mechanisms are the protocols used by blockchain networks to agree on the state of the ledger. Proof of Work requires participants to solve complex mathematical puzzles, while Proof of Stake selects validators based on the amount of cryptocurrency they hold. Different mechanisms offer different tradeoffs between security and energy efficiency.

Privacy and scalability are significant challenges for blockchain technology. Public blockchains are transparent by design, which can be problematic for sensitive business data. Scaling blockchain networks to handle high transaction volumes remains an active area of research and development.
